# Phase & Performance: What the Evidence Actually Says

source_id: PHASE-EVIDENCE

## McNulty et al. 2020 — the largest meta-analysis to date

McNulty, K.L., et al. (2020), *Sports Medicine*, pooled 78 studies on menstrual cycle phase
and exercise performance — the largest meta-analysis on this question to date. It found that
exercise performance may be **trivially reduced** in the early follicular phase compared with
all other phases; performance was consistent between all other phases. The effect size was
trivial, between-study variation was large, and the overall evidence quality was rated **low**.
The authors explicitly concluded that **general guidelines across the menstrual cycle cannot be
formed** from this evidence, and that a **personalised approach**, based on each individual's own
response, is what is actually warranted.

## Colenso-Semple, D'Souza, Elliott-Sale & Phillips 2023 — umbrella review

This umbrella review of resistance-training evidence (*Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*,
2023) examined the existing body of literature on menstrual cycle phase and resistance training
outcomes. It found highly variable findings and poor, inconsistent methodology across the
literature. The authors concluded that it is **premature** to state that menstrual-cycle-phase
hormone fluctuations appreciably affect acute exercise performance or long-term strength and
hypertrophy adaptations.

## Niering et al. 2024 — maximal strength systematic review

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis focused specifically on maximal strength
(Niering et al., *Sports*), pooling 22 studies and 433 women, found **heterogeneous** results
across studies — no consistent pattern of strength being higher or lower in any particular
phase.

## The takeaway

There is no credible evidence base for prescribing a specific percentage load reduction based
on menstrual cycle phase. Any app or coach doing so is inventing the number. What the evidence
does support is listening to how you actually feel on a given day.

This is the foundational reason powHER adjusts recommendations off a self-reported energy tag,
not off calculated cycle phase. Phase is retained for context, education, and long-term personal
pattern detection only — never as the basis for a load number.
