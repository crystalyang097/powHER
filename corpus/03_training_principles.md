# Training Principles (2026 ACSM Position Stand)

source_id: TRAINING-PRINCIPLES

Grounded in the 2026 ACSM Position Stand, *Resistance Training Prescription for Muscle
Function, Hypertrophy, and Physical Performance in Healthy Adults* — an overview of 137
systematic reviews covering more than 30,000 participants.

## Strength

Loads at or above 80% of one-rep max, trained through a full range of motion, 2–3 sets per
exercise, key lifts placed early in the session while fresh, at least 2 sessions per week per
muscle group.

## Hypertrophy

Higher weekly training volume — at least 10 sets per muscle group per week — with attention to
the eccentric (lowering) phase of each lift for added stimulus.

## Power

Moderate loads, roughly 30–70% of one-rep max, executed with a fast, intentional concentric
(lifting) phase.

## Effort: you don't have to train to failure

Training to absolute failure is **not necessary** for strength or hypertrophy gains. Sufficient
training effort is achievable while stopping near failure — roughly 2–3 reps in reserve on
working sets. Pushing every set to complete failure adds fatigue without a proportional return
in adaptation.

## Any training beats no training

Non-traditional training — resistance bands, bodyweight work, home-based programs without a
full gym — produces marked, measurable strength and hypertrophy benefits. The largest gains of
all come from the jump between *no* resistance training and *any* resistance training at all,
regardless of how modest that training is.

These two findings — that training to failure isn't required, and that any training beats no
training — are the evidence base for powHER's entire gentleness thesis: a lighter or shorter
session is still real training, not a lesser one.
