# Cycle Phase Education

source_id: PHASE-EDUCATION

## Why this matters: the knowledge gap

A 2023 survey (*MDPI*) found that 70.1% of recreational female athletes were not knowledgeable
about their menstrual cycle phases, and 55.5% did not know which hormones are involved. Basic
cycle literacy is genuinely uncommon — not knowing this isn't a personal failing, it's the norm,
and it's rarely taught.

## Symptom prevalence

Across 1,086 athletes surveyed in 57 sports (*Frontiers in Physiology*, 2022), dysmenorrhea
(painful periods) and PMS were the most commonly reported symptoms, and were perceived by
athletes to negatively affect aerobic fitness, muscle strength, mental sharpness, balance, and
sleep. Separately, dysmenorrhea is reported by 71–83% of female athletes — it is closer to a
norm than an exception.

A 2025 daily-monitoring study of 108 elite athletes across 554 cycles found that symptoms
clustered around menstruation and the few days before it, and correlated with reduced
well-being. Critically, the study found that **performance declined on symptomatic days, not on
phase days** — it was how someone felt, not which phase the calendar said she was in, that
predicted a harder day. This distinction is the empirical backbone of powHER's symptom-first
design: recommendations respond to your reported energy and symptoms, not to a phase label.

## Menstrual phase (roughly days 1–5)

This is when bleeding occurs; estrogen and progesterone are both at their lowest. Some women
feel fatigued, crampy, or low-energy during this window. Some feel completely normal. Exercise —
at whatever intensity feels right — is evidence-backed to reduce menstrual pain, not something to
avoid. Some women notice this. Many notice nothing. Both are completely normal.

## Follicular phase (roughly day 6 through just before ovulation)

Estrogen rises through this window as the body prepares to ovulate. Some women report feeling
more energetic as this phase progresses. The evidence for a consistent performance boost here is
weak and inconsistent — see the phase-evidence corpus for detail. Some women notice this. Many
notice nothing. Both are completely normal.

## Ovulatory phase (roughly the 3 days around mid-cycle)

Estrogen peaks and then drops sharply around ovulation, with a brief LH surge. Some women report
a short window of feeling strong or energized here. Others notice nothing distinct at all. Some
women notice this. Many notice nothing. Both are completely normal.

## Luteal phase (from ovulation to the end of the cycle)

Progesterone rises, then both hormones fall sharply in the days just before the next period if
pregnancy hasn't occurred — this pre-menstrual window is when PMS symptoms are most commonly
reported. Some women notice bloating, mood changes, fatigue, or breast tenderness here. Others
notice little to nothing. Some women notice this. Many notice nothing. Both are completely
normal.
