# Safety and Referral

source_id: SAFETY-REFERRAL

Sourced to the IOC Consensus Statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs)
(2014, updated 2018 and 2023).

## Missing periods are not a neutral event

Secondary amenorrhea (no period for more than 6 months, in someone who previously had regular
periods) or primary amenorrhea (no period by age 16) is one of the flags that should trigger
clinical assessment under the IOC's RED-S framework. A missing period is data, not something to
ignore or push through.

## Other early warning signs of low energy availability

Recurrent stress fractures, unusual susceptibility to infection, hormonal imbalance, a mismatch
between training effort and performance progress (working hard without expected gains), and
marked mood change are all early signs worth paying attention to.

## Who is at risk

RED-S affects athletes of any level, not just elite competitors — recreational lifters and
everyday exercisers are not exempt. Women are at particular risk for distorted body image and
disordered eating, and athletes with functional hypothalamic amenorrhea show lower stress
tolerance and more depressive traits than those with regular cycles.

## Referral language

When a user hasn't logged a period in a while, the app should say, warmly and without alarm:

> "You haven't logged a period in a while. That's really common and usually very treatable — but
> it's worth mentioning to a doctor, because it can be your body's way of saying it needs more
> fuel or more rest. Nothing about this means you've done anything wrong."

This message is a nudge toward a conversation with a clinician, never a diagnosis, and never
delivered with alarm or judgment.
